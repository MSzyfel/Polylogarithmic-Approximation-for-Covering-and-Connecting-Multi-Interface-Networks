\section{Introduction}
Designing Internet of Things (IoT) networks often demands establishing connection between large number of devices, which can be achieved by choosing between large variety of interfaces available at each device. Moreover, the devices present in the network may vary according to the interfaces they provide for the usage. For example some device may be able to connect by Bluetooth and Wi-Fi, while the others may provide Zigbee and Z-Wave interfaces. Since IoT devices are typically resource-constrained, it may be desirable to try to establish every relevant connection, while minimizing the amount of resources required to do so.

We model the above problem as a graph problem. 
Every device in the network is modeled by a vertex with a set of labels representing its available interfaces. 
Two devices can communicate if they share a common available interface and are sufficiently close to each other, and the edges of the graph represent the possible connections. 
A connection is established between two devices when they share a common active interface. 
We want to find an assignment of active interfaces so that we ensure either coverage or connectivity of the network.
In the coverage problem, we wish to establish every possible connection in the network. 
To see an example, see Figure~\ref{example_covering}. 
In the connectivity problem, we are additionally given a subset of vertices called terminals, an we require that terminals are part of the same connected component in the subgraph induced by active interfaces. 
To see an example, see Figure~\ref{example_connecting}. 
In both problems, we assume that activating an interface induces a cost that depends on the type of interface and on the vertex that activates it. 
The goal is to minimize either the min-sum cost (the sum of costs over all vertices) or the min-max cost (the maximum cost in a vertex).

\subsection{Related Work}

\subparagraph*{Coverage Problem.}
The algorithmic study of problems in multi-interface networks started with the \emph{coverage} problem, introduced in Caporuscio et al. \cite{EnergeticPerformanceOfServiceOrientedMultiRadioNetworks}. 
The coverage problem has been studied only in the \emph{homogeneous} setting, when costs depend on the type of interface but not on the vertex. It has been studied both for min-sum \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks} and min-max \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks} objective functions. 

The min-sum variant is APX-hard for any constant $k \geq 3$ and unit costs, where $k$ is the number of different interfaces in the network, and it is hard to approximate within a $O\br{\ln k}$ factor even with unit costs, but it has a $\min \brc{k-1, \sqrt{n} \br{1+\ln n}}$-approximation \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks}.

The min-max variant is \NPhard for any $\Delta \geq 5$, $k \geq 16$ and unit costs, where $\Delta$ is the maximum degree of the input graph, and it is not approximable within a $\eta \ln \Delta$ factor for some constant $\eta$ even on trees and with unit costs. On the positive side, it admits a $\br{\ln \Delta +1 +b \cdot \min \brc{c_{\max}, \ln \Delta +1}}$-approximation, where $c_{\max}$ is the maximum cost of an interface and $b$ is a parameter that depends on the structural properties of the input graph. 
It also has a $(1+\br{k-2} \frac{c_{\max}}{2 c_{\min}})$-approximation and a $(\frac{\Delta}{2} \frac{c_{\max}}{c_{\min}})$-approximation \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}. 

\subparagraph*{Connectivity Problem.} The \emph{connectivity} problem was introduced by Kosowski et al. \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}. 
It was first studied in the homogeneous setting and in the special case where all vertices are terminals, both for the min-sum \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths} and the min-max objective functions \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

The min-sum variant with terminals only and homogeneous costs is APX-hard for $k \geq 2$, $\Delta \geq 4$ and unit costs already, but has a $2$-approximation \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}.
This result was improved by Athanassopoulos et al. \cite{EnergyEfficientCommunicationInMultiInterfaceWirelessNetworks} to a randomized $(\frac{3}{2} + \epsilon)$-approximation for any constant $\epsilon$. 
Moreover, this approximation algorithm applies to the more general problem where for each interface, we are given the set of edges that it can cover.

The min-max variant with terminals only and homogeneous costs is \NPhard for any $\Delta \geq 3$, $k \geq 10$ and unit costs.
Similarly as the coverage problem, it is not approximable within a $\eta \ln n$ factor even for unit costs, but has simple $(1+(k-2) \frac{c_{\max}}{2 c_{\min}})$- and $\frac{\Delta}{2} \frac{c_{\max}}{c_{\min}}$-approximations \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

Athanassopoulos et al. \cite{EnergyEfficientCommunicationInMultiInterfaceWirelessNetworks} expand the study of the min-sum connectivity problem to \emph{group connectivity}, a more general problem where the solution graph should connect together groups of vertices that might not span the whole set of vertices. 
They provide a randomized $4$-approximation for it. 
They also initiate the study of this problem in the \emph{heterogeneous} setting, where costs depend on the interface and the vertex. 
They show that the heterogeneous connectivity problem is hard to approximate within $o(\ln n)$, but the group connectivity heterogeneous case admits a $O(\ln n)$-approximation.

\subparagraph*{Other Multi-interface Problems.} There has been some work on the study of the coverage problem from the perspective of parameterized complexity, possibly with an addtional constraint on the maximum number of active interfaces in a vertex \cite{EnergyConsumptionBalancingInMultiInterfaceNetworks,MinMaxCoverageInMultiInterfaceNetworksPathwidth,OnComputationalComplexityOfCoveringMultiInterfaceNetworks}, yielding efficient algorithms in some graph classes.

Some papers also study other problems in multi-interface networks, like cheapest path \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}, matchings \cite{MaximumMatchingInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, or flow problems \cite{FlowProblemsInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

\subsection{Our contribution and techniques}
In this paper we give a first $O\br{\log m}$-approximation algorithm for the min-max connection problem with non-uniform costs. This improves the previously known result of \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, which gave an $O\br{\ln\Delta+b\cdot\min\brc{\ln \Delta,c_{\text{max}}}}$-approximation for the min-max covering version, where $b$ is the parameter related to the existence of a $b$-owner function \cite{SemidynamicAlgorithmsForMaintainingSingleSourceShortestPathTrees,PlanarOrientationsWithLowOutDegreeAndCompactionOfAdjacencyMatrices}. This als improves the $O\br{\sqrt{n}\cdot\log n}$-approximation for the min-sum covering problem by \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks}. Our approach is based on a set-cover-style LP relaxation together with a randomized rounding. We also show how to use the same LP relaxation to obtain a $k$-approximation algorithm for the min-sum and min-max covering problems, where $k$ is the number of interfaces. All these guarantees hold in the non-uniform setting where activation costs depend on both the interface and the vertex it is assigned to. 

% Then, we shift to combinatorial algorithms. We give a $\Delta$-approximation algorithm for min-sum covering, a $1.39\Delta$-approximation algorithm for min-sum connection, and a $2\Delta$-approximation algorithm for min-max covering, where $\Delta$ is the maximum degree of the input graph. In particular, for min-max covering this improves on the previously known $O\br{\Delta\cdot c_{\text{max}}}$ approximation, where $c_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum interface-activation cost.

We also prove that even the unit-cost min-sum covering assignment problem on bipartite graphs cannot be approximated within a factor of $o\br{\log \Delta}$ unless $P=NP$. Since analogous logarithmic hardness is known for min-max variants of the problems \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, our $O\br{\log m}$-approximation guarantees are tight up to constant factors for the case when $\Delta$ is unbounded.
