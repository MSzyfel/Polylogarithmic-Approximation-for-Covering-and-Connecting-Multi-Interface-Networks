\section{Introduction}
Designing Internet of Things (IoT) networks often demands establishing connection between large number of devices, which can be achieved by choosing between large variety of interfaces available at each device. Moreover, the devices present in the network may vary according to the interfaces they provide for the usage. For example some device may be able to connect by Bluetooth and Wi-Fi, while the others may provide Zigbee and Z-Wave interfaces. Since IoT devices are typically resource-constrained, it may be desirable to try to establish every relevant connection, while minimizing the amount of resources required to do so.

We model the above problem as a graph problem. Every device in the network is modeled by a vertex with a set of labels representing its available interfaces. Two devices can communicate if they share a common available interface and are sufficiently close to each other, and the edges of the graph represent the possible connections. A connection is established between two devices when they share a common active interface. 
We want to find an assignment of active interfaces so that we ensure either \textsc{Coverage} or \textsc{Connectivity}. In the \textsc{Coverage} problem, we wish to establish every possible connection in the network (see Figure~\ref{example_covering} for an example) while in the \textsc{Connectivity} problem, we are additionally given a collection of disjoint vertex subsets called terminals, and we require each terminal group to be connected in the subgraph induced by active interfaces (see Figure~\ref{example_connecting} for an example). In both problems, we assume that activating an interface induces a cost that depends on the type of interface and on the vertex that activates it. The goal is to minimize the min-max cost, i.~e., the maximum cost of activated interfaces across all vertices.

\subsection{Related Work}

In the following, we denote $k$ the number of different interfaces across the network, $n$ the number of vertices, $\Delta$ the maximum degree of the graph, $c_{\max}$ the maximum cost and $c_{\min}$ the minimum cost of an interface. In the \emph{homogeneous} setting, the costs depend on the interface only while in the \emph{heterogeneous} case they depend on the interface and on the vertex. 

\subparagraph*{\textsc{Coverage} Problem.}
The algorithmic study of problems in multi-interface networks started with the \textsc{Coverage} problem, introduced in Caporuscio et al. \cite{EnergeticPerformanceOfServiceOrientedMultiRadioNetworks}. 
The \textsc{Coverage} problem has been studied only in the homogeneous setting, both for min-sum \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks} and min-max \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks} objective functions. 

The min-sum variant is APX-hard for any constant $k \geq 3$ and unit costs, and it is hard to approximate within a $O\br{\ln k}$ factor even with unit costs, but it has a $\min \brc{k-1, \sqrt{n} \br{1+\ln n}}$-approximation \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks}.

The min-max variant is \NPhard for any constant $\Delta \geq 5$, $k \geq 16$ and for unit costs, and it is not approximable within a $\eta \ln \Delta$ factor for some constant $\eta$ even on trees and with unit costs. On the positive side, it admits a $\br{\ln \Delta +1 +b \cdot \min \brc{c_{\max}, \ln \Delta +1}}$-approximation, where $b$ is a parameter that depends on the structural properties of the input graph and is bounded by many other graph structutal parameters such as treewidth, arboricity or maximum degree. It also has a $(1+\br{k-2} \frac{c_{\max}}{2 c_{\min}})$-approximation and a $(\frac{\Delta}{2} \frac{c_{\max}}{c_{\min}})$-approximation \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}. 

\subparagraph*{\textsc{Connectivity} Problem.} The \textsc{Connectivity} problem was introduced by Kosowski et al. \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}. It was first studied in the homogeneous setting and in the special case where all vertices are terminals, both for the min-sum \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths} and the min-max objective functions \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

The min-sum variant with terminals only and homogeneous costs is APX-hard for $k \geq 2$, $\Delta \geq 4$ and unit costs already, but has a $2$-approximation \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}. This result was improved by Athanassopoulos et al. \cite{EnergyEfficientCommunicationInMultiInterfaceWirelessNetworks} to a randomized $(\frac{3}{2} + \epsilon)$-approximation for any constant $\epsilon$. Moreover, this approximation algorithm applies to the more general problem where for each interface, we are additionally given the set of edges that it can cover.

The min-max variant where costs are homogeneous and all vertices are terminals is \NPhard for any constant $\Delta \geq 3$, $k \geq 10$ and for unit costs. Similarly to the \textsc{Coverage} problem, it is not approximable within a $\eta \ln n$ factor even for unit costs, but has simple $(1+(k-2) \frac{c_{\max}}{2 c_{\min}})$- and $\frac{\Delta}{2} \frac{c_{\max}}{c_{\min}}$-approximations \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

Athanassopoulos et al. \cite{EnergyEfficientCommunicationInMultiInterfaceWirelessNetworks} expand the study of the min-sum \textsc{Connectivity} problem to \emph{group connectivity}, a more general problem where the solution graph should connect together groups of vertices that might not span the whole set of vertices. They provide a randomized $4$-approximation for it. They also initiate the study of this problem in the heterogeneous setting, where costs can also depend on the vertex. They show that the heterogeneous \textsc{Connectivity} problem is hard to approximate within $o(\ln n)$, but the group connectivity heterogeneous case admits a $O(\ln n)$-approximation.

\subparagraph*{Other Multi-interface Problems.} There has been some work on the study of the \textsc{Coverage} problem from the perspective of parameterized complexity, possibly with an addtional constraint on the maximum number of active interfaces in a vertex \cite{EnergyConsumptionBalancingInMultiInterfaceNetworks,MinMaxCoverageInMultiInterfaceNetworksPathwidth,OnComputationalComplexityOfCoveringMultiInterfaceNetworks}, yielding efficient algorithms in some graph classes. Some papers also study other problems in multi-interface networks, like cheapest path \cite{ExploitingMultiInterfaceNetworksConnectivityAndCheapestPaths}, matchings \cite{MaximumMatchingInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, or flow problems \cite{FlowProblemsInMultiInterfaceNetworks}.

\subsection{Our contribution and techniques}

We study the min-max \textsc{Coverage} and \textsc{Connectivity} problems in the heterogeneous setting, where activation costs may depend on both the interface and the vertex. We give a set-cover-style LP relaxation for the min-max \textsc{Coverage} problem and show that treating the resulting fractional solution as a probability distribution and applying a randomized rounding procedure yields an $O\br{\log m}$-approximation, where $m$ is the number of edges in the graph. This is the first logarithmic approximation for this problem with heterogeneous costs, and it matches the previously known lower bound of $\Omega\br{\log n}$ for unit costs and on stars \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}. It also improves the previously best known $O\br{\ln \Delta + b \cdot \min\brc{\ln \Delta, c_{\max}}}$ approximation ratio \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, which was limited to the homogeneous case. 
A simpler deterministic rounding of the same LP gives a $k$-approximation, where $k$ is the number of interfaces, both for the min-sum and min-max coverage problem. The existing algorithms for these two problem have approximation ratio $O\br{k-1}$ for min-sum \cite{CostMinimizationInWirelessNetworks} and $O\br{1+(k-2) \frac{c_{\max}}{2 c_{\min}}}$ for min-max \cite{MinimizeTheMaximumDutyInMultiInterfaceNetworks}, but they work only for homogeneous costs, while our algorithm applies to the heterogeneous setting.

For the min-max \textsc{Connectivity} problem, we extend the LP by adding flow-based cut constraints encoding group connectivity. Our rounding procedure is similar to the one for the min-max \textsc{Coverage} problem, however, this time our rounding is iterative and the algorithm proceeds until all terminal groups are connected. Each iteration is randomized and we show that in expectation in each such round the algorithm covers a siginificant fraction of edges of some graph which connects all group terminals with high probability. This yields an $O\br{\log^2 m}$-approximation algorithm, which is the first non-trivial approximation algorithm for this problem.
